Green Hydrogen

Electrolytic hydrogen replacing fossil feedstocks and fuel

TRL 7–9 · Mid-term

How it works

Renewable electricity powers electrolysers to split water into hydrogen and oxygen. Green hydrogen replaces natural gas as feedstock in ammonia synthesis (Haber-Bosch process) and as fuel in high-temperature industrial processes.

Applicability in Bulgaria

  • Ammonia production (Agropolychim, Neochim) - largest near-term opportunity
  • Refinery hydrotreating units (LUKOIL Neftohim Burgas)
  • High-temperature heat for chemical processes
  • Agropolychim: blue ammonia integrated by 2026; green ammonia by 2030 (confirmed in company document)
  • First low-carbon ammonia delivery by Agropolychim: saves 5,450 tonnes CO₂ (document confirmed)

CCUS

Carbon Capture, Utilisation and Storage

TRL 6–8 · Long-term

How it works

CO₂ is captured from industrial process streams or flue gases, transported via pipeline, and stored permanently in geological formations (saline aquifers, depleted gas fields) or used as feedstock for chemicals and materials.

Bulgaria's CCUS potential

  • Estimated storage capacity: ≥2.6 Gt CO₂ (≈79 years of current emissions)
  • Offshore Black Sea storage (Galata depleted gas field)
  • Onshore saline aquifers
  • Devnya/Varna industrial cluster - ideal for shared CO₂ transport

Key projects

ANRAV
€600M assumed CCUS CAPEX split into €190M funded by the EU Innovation Fund (incl. €38M for geological storage) and €410M remaining project financing; 800,000 t CO₂/yr captured at Devnya (Heidelberg Materials); hybrid OxyCal + post-combustion technology; ANRAV.beta pilot completed; full-scale commissioning 2028. Process Electrification

Replacing fossil fuel heat with renewable electricity

TRL 5–9 · Near-term

How it works

Electric heat pumps, resistance heaters, plasma furnaces and induction systems replace gas/coal-fired process heat. Most effective for low- and medium-temperature processes (<400°C) but advancing for high-temperature applications.

Sector applicability

  • Low-heat processes across most chemical sites - near-term opportunity
  • Ceramics: electrified firing where product and kiln allow
  • Constrained by grid capacity and electricity price in Bulgaria
  • Bulgaria's NECP does not yet propose specific electrification policies for industry

Barriers & enablers

Barrier
High electricity prices; grid capacity limitations; high capital cost of electrification retrofits
Enabler
Expanding renewables (55.51% RES electricity target by 2030); dedicated industrial electricity tariffs; EU funding
Policy gap
NECP lacks concrete electrification targets for industry - policy gap identified by CSD (2025)

Biomass & Alternative Fuels

Replacing fossil heat with biomass residues, RDF, tyres, biogas, and other qualified alternative fuels

TRL 8–9 · Near-term

How it works

Biomass and alternative fuels displace coal, petcoke, natural gas, and other fossil fuels in boilers, kilns, and steam systems. Eligible biomass residues are treated as biogenic under IPCC accounting, while RDF, tyres, biogas, and process residues lower fossil fuel demand and reduce exposure to ETS costs.

Current deployments

  • Solvay Sodi: biomass co-firing is already around 30% in one boiler, delivering about 20% CO₂ reduction; the next step is scale-up to 50% in CFB steam generators No. 7 and No. 8.
  • Svilosa: BioTEC biomass CHP is operational at 16 MW and 65 t steam/hour from waste biomass; future HERB boiler modernization would improve black liquor combustion and may enable BECCS later.
  • Biovet / Huvepharma: Greenburn® treats residual mycelium as biomass for carbon-neutral steam, with biogas from wastewater fermentation; the site reports 30% absolute CO₂ reduction over 2020–2023.
  • Holcim Bulgaria: approximately 90% fossil fuel substitution in clinker production using alternative fuels, contributing to 443 kg CO₂/t cement in 2023, the lowest specific emissions in the global Holcim Group.
  • Heidelberg Materials: RDF and tyre-feeding upgrade on kiln Line No. 7, including a 480 t/day shredder, supports about 60 kt CO₂/year reduction.

Deployment logic

Best fit
Steam boilers, cement kilns, pulp and paper recovery systems, and sites with stable biomass or waste-derived fuel supply.
Constraints
Sustainable biomass availability, fuel preparation, permitting, air-emission controls, and avoiding competition with higher-value material uses.
Bridge role
Alternative fuels can cut fossil emissions immediately while electrification, hydrogen, and CCUS mature for harder process emissions.

Circular Economy

Reducing virgin material demand through waste valorization, bio-based products, and process redesign

TRL 5–9 · Phased

How it works

Circular routes reduce emissions by keeping carbon and materials in productive use for longer. The priority is to turn industrial by-products, biomass side streams, agricultural residues, and lower-value waste into higher-value products or process inputs instead of relying on virgin fossil or mineral feedstocks.

Specific opportunities

  • Svilosa: microcrystalline cellulose installation above €13.1M creates higher-value products from biomass and lowers emissions intensity per unit of output.
  • Orgachim: sunflower- and castor-oil-based coatings and resins support bio-based, low-VOC product development and lower dependence on fossil-derived inputs.
  • Solvay Sodi: e.Solvay electrochemical process under testing could reduce CO₂ by about 50% while saving water, salt, and limestone.
  • Solvay Sodi: bio-coke testing replaces metallurgical coke with wood charcoal in lime kilns as a circular bridge before full electrification.
  • Fertilizers: sulfur-containing complex fertilizers and soil conditioners can be produced from selected industrial, agricultural, and waste-derived streams.

Value levers

Products
Bio-based coatings, cellulose derivatives, low-VOC formulations, and specialty products improve value per tonne of feedstock.
Processes
Electrochemical production and process-water savings cut both direct emissions and resource intensity.
Waste streams
Organic, inorganic, agricultural, and industrial residues become inputs for fertilizers, soil conditioners, nano-products, and specialty materials where quality and safety specifications allow.

N₂O Abatement

Nitrous oxide destruction at nitric acid plants

TRL 9 · Ready now

How it works

Catalytic N₂O destruction is installed in the tail-gas stream of nitric acid plants. N₂O (a greenhouse gas 265× more potent than CO₂ over 100 years) is decomposed into nitrogen and oxygen before release. This represents the highest-impact, lowest-cost decarbonization measure available to the fertilizer sector.

Why it matters for Bulgaria

  • Nitric acid plants: Agropolychim (Devnya) and Neochim (Dimitrovgrad)
  • N₂O abatement progressively adopted as Best Available Technique under EU IED
  • Cost-effective: high reduction efficiency at relatively low investment
  • Unlocks ETS carbon credits - economic benefit

Impact

90%+
N₂O removal efficiency
Low cost
Lowest abatement cost per tonne CO₂eq in fertilizers
Ready
Mature technology - no regulatory barriers
Priority action: N₂O abatement on nitric acid units is the most immediate "no-regrets" measure in Bulgaria's chemical sector. Agropolychim integrates near-zero N₂O into new HNO₃ plant design (€250M programme). Neochim urgently needs catalytic abatement installation: €3–6M → 277 kt CO₂eq/yr saved → payback <3 months.

N₂O saving calculation

Cashflow model
Capex and CO₂ figures from company documents. Cashflow uses avoided ETS compliance cost, not full ETS price: avoided CO₂ × ETS price × paid ETS share. CBAM-sector paid share follows the official free-allocation phase-out. Non-CBAM sectors use a separate explicit free-allocation assumption. Optional CBAM adjustment is a market proxy, not a legal CBAM cost for EU producers.
IRR signal thresholds
>10% IRR: payback <7 yr. 5–10%: payback 7–14 yr. <5%: payback >14 yr. Near-zero or negative: >20 yr. Consistent with US DOE Chemicals Liftoff (>10% IRR threshold).
ROI Calculation Formulas
Notation: CAPEX is in M€, CO₂ savings are in ktCO₂e/year, ETS and CBAM certificate prices are €/tCO₂, and all annual outputs are in M€ unless stated otherwise.
Pathway annual ETS value
annual_saving_y = CO2_saved_kt × ETS_price_y / 1000
Used for the payback chart, ROI table 2026/2030 savings, sensitivity table, and simple pathway screening.
Payback period
payback_years = CAPEX_MEUR / annual_saving_commissioning_year
If a pathway starts before 2026, the 2026 ETS price is used. Pathways with zero direct CO₂ savings are shown as enablers.
Net by 2040
net_2040 = Σ annual_saving_y - CAPEX_MEUR
The sum starts in the commissioning year and runs through 2040. This is the net value shown in the ROI metrics table.
Marginal abatement cost
break_even_price = CAPEX_EUR / annual_CO2_saved_t
Shown as €/tCO₂ in the MAC chart. A pathway below the ETS threshold line is cheaper than paying the carbon price on the same tonnes.
Cashflow CAPEX deployment
investment_y = CAPEX_MEUR × capex_fraction_y
Cumulative investment is Σ investment_y. The chart plots this as a negative line so outflows are visible below zero.
Cashflow ETS avoided compliance cost
ets_savings_y = CO2_saved_kt × ETS_price_y × paid_ETS_share_y / 1000
This is the cashflow-specific savings formula. CBAM-sector installations use the official CBAM-linked free-allocation phase-out; non-CBAM sectors use the separate free-allocation assumption.
Paid ETS share
paid_ETS_share_y = CBAM_schedule_y OR non_CBAM_assumption_y
CBAM-sector schedule: 2.5% in 2026, 5% in 2027, 10% in 2028, 22.5% in 2029, 48.5% in 2030, 61% in 2031, 73.5% in 2032, 86% in 2033, 100% from 2034. Non-CBAM sectors use the explicit model assumption in code.
Optional CBAM market proxy
cbam_proxy_y = volume_t × emission_factor × certificate_price_y × CBAM_paid_share_y × pass_through_share / 1,000,000
Only included when “Include CBAM market adjustment” is ON. It is a market adjustment proxy, not a direct legal CBAM cost for EU producers.
Cumulative net cashflow
net_position_y = Σ[(ets_savings_y + optional_cbam_proxy_y) - investment_y]
Break-even occurs when cumulative net position reaches or crosses zero.
Signed log y-axis
chart_value = sign(x) × log10(1 + abs(x))
Used only when the cashflow log toggle is ON, so positive and negative values remain visible on the same axis. Tooltips show the original M€ values.
N₂O abatement input
CO2e_saved_kt = HNO3_output_t × N2O_reduced_kg_per_t × GWP100 / 1,000,000
Used to derive nitric-acid abatement pathway inputs where production and N₂O reduction factors are the underlying source data.
IRR signal proxy
IRR_signal = threshold(payback_years)
>10% IRR if payback is below 7 years; 5–10% if 7–14 years; <5% if 14–20 years; very long-term above 20 years.
Scenario and KPI aggregation
annual_carbon_savings_2030 = Σ(CO2_saved_kt × ETS_price_2030 / 1000)
The 2030 savings KPI sums all pathways commissioned by 2030 under the selected ETS price scenario. Company-level cashflow aggregates sum annual investment, ETS savings, CBAM proxy, and net position across the selected companies and enabled technology groups.

Methodology & Assumptions
CBAM phase-in schedule
Official EU schedule: 2.5% (2026) → 5% → 10% → 22.5% → 48.5% → 61% → 73.5% → 86% → 100% (2034). Fertilizers/NH₃ in scope from 2026. Source: Climat.be
ETS price scenarios
Base: Reuters analyst consensus Jan 2026 (€85–145/t). Low: conservative Enerdata scenario. High: GMK Centre upper range. Source: Reuters/GMK Centre
Emission factors
AN/CAN: 1.18 tCO₂/t (EBRD 2023). NH₃: 1.57 tCO₂/t (official CBAM benchmark, Argus/EC). HNO₃: 0.23 tCO₂/t. Soda ash: 0.90 tCO₂/t. Refining: 0.42 tCO₂/t. Cement and non-CBAM chemical product factors are indicative proxies for dashboard screening.
Scope & limitations
Official CBAM sectors are Cement, Steel, Aluminium, Fertilizers, Electricity, and Hydrogen. In this dashboard, fertilizer/NH₃ and cement exposure is modeled directly; soda ash, petroleum, pulp, pharmaceutical products, and resins are shown as ETS/non-CBAM exposure proxies unless future CBAM extensions apply. Volumes are user inputs. Results are indicative, not compliance-grade.
